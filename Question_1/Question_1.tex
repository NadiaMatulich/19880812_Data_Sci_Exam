\section{Question 1: Covid}\label{question-1-covid}}

Having been directed by editor of the New York Times, the following
needs to be done:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  African experience of covid, vs rest of the world
\item
  Asssess co-morbidities on a Macro level

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Did countries with higher rates of co-morbidities fare worse?
  \end{itemize}
\item
  Hospitalisation:

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    How quickly did different regions increase their hospitalisation
    facilities
  \item
    As well as whether this lagged or led ICU emissions
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

If we compare different regions we obtain the following:

\includegraphics{Question_1_files/figure-latex/unnamed-chunk-1-1.pdf}

\includegraphics{Question_1_files/figure-latex/unnamed-chunk-2-1.pdf}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Africa experienced the highest death rates (per 1mil population) but
  managed to contain the spread of covid quite well.
\item
  Europe experienced the highest incidence of cases but exhibited a much
  lower rate of deaths per 1000000 population.
\end{itemize}

At the start of the pandemic, a lot was said about the effect of
co-morbidities on mortality.

\includegraphics{Question_1_files/figure-latex/unnamed-chunk-3-1.pdf}

\includegraphics{Question_1_files/figure-latex/Deaths By Co Morbidities-1.pdf}

\includegraphics{Question_1_files/figure-latex/Deaths By Co Morbidities 2-1.pdf}

\includegraphics{Question_1_files/figure-latex/Deaths By Co Morbidities 3-1.pdf}

we see that : * in countries with low cardio vascular death prevalence,
there are high covid deaths * this is likely due to high cardio death
prevalence being linked to a poor hospital system with poor record
keeping * as cardio vascular deaths prevalence increases, deaths
decrease, then increase again (then decrease again) * initial decrease
likely due to better record keeping * subsequent increase due to
underlying co-morbities * subsequent decrease due to lack of data at the
end of the graph (hence high confidence intervals)
